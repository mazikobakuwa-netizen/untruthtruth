Immigration

Without a doubt, the rhetoric used to describe people who come to another country is heavy and complex, carrying different legal implications. Words like "illegal alien," "undocumented immigrant," "asylum seeker," and "migrant" all have different implications and appeal differently to everyone. This collection examines how the rhetoric concerning new immigration policies in the U.S. are represented.

Economist:Welcome to Zero Migration America

Source Link
Examines the effect of the immigration policy changes on the U.S. population and economy. This article equates little to no immigration or stricter restrictions to a poorer country with less innovation, analyzing the effect of the moves in the 1900's following 1930, until now.
Bias: Low Favors: Neutral

DEUTSCHE WELLE: MAGA and Immigration - In the Shadow of Trump's Border

Source Link
Discusses the impact of immigration policy changes in the United Staes, by interviewing immigrants (including asylum seekers), children of immigrants, and supporters of the immigration policy. Uses rhetoric such as "brutal crackdown on immigration," jeopardy" while referring to an asylum seekers status, and "U.S.'s struggle," showing empathy to both sides of the crisis' impact.
Bias: High Favors: Mexico

El Universal: Mass deportations from the United States to Mexico

Source Link
Details the history of immigration from the U.S. to Mexico with history of previos treaties, and how their stipulations differ from the current administrations policies. Reflects om the strain caused to Mexican and all Latin American natives by the policies enforcement with the actions of ICE, using language like "strain," "pain," and "forced labor," to invoke empathy.
Bias: Medium Favors: Mexico
